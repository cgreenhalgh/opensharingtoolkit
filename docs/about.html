About Open Sharing Toolkit

Chris Greenhalgh, The University of Nottingham, 2013

The toolkit is intended to allow sharing of media files and applications on and off the Internet. The toolkit aims to support a broad range of media types, but has an emphasis on the social and institutional embedding of technologies and media.The toolkit itself can also be reproduced and passed on to others, including off-internet peer distribution.

The toolkit targets a range of devices and device types:

  • hubs - (typically) small servers, that can be deployed in strategic locations to provide local access to media and services.
    • a hub will usually support several means of access/interaction, including Bluetooth, WiFi, NFC, USB, SMS, GSM voice
    • a hub may also provide internet connectivity;
    • a hub may be cloud-hosted, but it that case it can only provide services/media to internet connected devices (or will require the use of a 3rd part gateway, e.g. for voice, SMS interaction).
  • smart phones and tablets, with an initial emphasis on Android devices, which can become full or limited hubs in their own right
  • cell phones, which are a critical class of (mass-availability) client device, including 'dumb' phones, feature phones, smart phones
  • other client devices, e.g. laptops, PCs

Initially the toolkit will be an assembly of (often pointers to) third party open source applications (mainly for Linux and Android) and instructions plus potentially useful/interesting data and media files.

The first explorations (prior to August 2013) targetted Android devices in particular and a peer-to-peer pattern of operation. The addition of Hubs introduces more asymetry into the design with the intention of providing richer services to cell phone clients. It is anticipated that Linux and Android will be target OSs for the hub.
